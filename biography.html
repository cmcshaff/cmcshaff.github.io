About

Biography

I am a theoretical and systems biologist completing my Ph.D. in the Cognitive Science Program at Indiana University Bloomington, advised by Randall D. Beer, and a National Science Foundation Graduate Research Fellow. I work on the viability of living systems: the physiological limits that divide life from death, and how an agent's behavior and metabolism play out inside them.

My main line of work develops viability space decomposition, a framework for analyzing dynamical models of agents that must respect viability constraints to stay alive. It derives global manifolds — mortality, ordering, and collapse — that carve a model's state space into regions of similar survival outcomes, a picture I call a viability portrait. Alongside this, I study intrinsic viability: how an organism's constraints can arise from within, using idealized self-maintaining systems such as gliders in Conway's Game of Life.

From cognitive science to the living

I came to this through cognitive science, which I think of broadly as the study of autonomous systems behaving in their environments — minds wherever we find them. Reading Maturana and Varela convinced me that the constitution of living systems and their cognitive dynamics could be joined in a single formal theory, and that conviction is what pulls my work toward biology, artificial life, and dynamical systems at once.

What's next

In Fall 2026 I join the Vivarium Lab at the Richard D. Berlin Center for Cell Analysis and Modeling (UConn Health) as a postdoctoral fellow, working on compositional modeling and viability. I'm also a co-founder and board member of the Agora Institute for Integrative Biology.

Outside the equations

Away from the models, I photograph soap-film interference through a microscope — thin films of color captured in the seconds before they drain and pop. That work lives in the gallery. It scratches the same itch as the research: structure made briefly visible in something otherwise invisible.
